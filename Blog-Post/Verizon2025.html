Cybersecurity as a Business Risk: Key Takeaways from Verizon’s 2025 DBIR

The tech world always pays attention when Verizon releases its annual Data Breach Investigations Report (DBIR). Analyzing 22,052 real-world security incidents across 139 countries, this massive report offers an unparalleled look at what security breaches are most common, what is creating corporate vulnerabilities, and exactly where threat actors are striking.

At Midwest IT Provider, we look at the DBIR not just as a collection of technical data, but as a roadmap for protecting our clients. The single most important takeaway from this year’s report is that cybersecurity is now a business risk conversation, not an IT conversation.

Modern corporate boards, business owners, and CFOs are all asking the same foundational questions: Are we doing enough? Is our strategy working? How do we know?

To help you answer those questions, we have pulled out the three most critical, actionable findings from the 2025 DBIR—and what your business needs to do to address them.

1. The Skyrocketing Danger of “Third-Party” Vendor Risk

For years, the percentage of data breaches involving third parties has crept upward. However, the latest data reveals an astonishing surge: third parties were involved in 30% of all security breaches. This figure is exactly double what it was the previous year (15%).

When your business integrates external software or support, you are inadvertently inheriting their security limitations. This “vendor risk” routinely slips into corporate networks via:

  • SaaS applications hosting proprietary company data.
  • Public or Private Cloud services hosting company servers.
  • Unsecured Managed Service Providers (MSPs) with back-door access to internal networks.

According to the DBIR, 81% of these third-party breaches involved “system intrusion,” frequently driven by hackers reusing stolen credentials in an external environment. As the report dryly notes, “Managing credentials will be harder in an environment you don’t control.”

Our Recommended Three-Pronged Defense:

To insulate your infrastructure from vulnerable partners, we help businesses implement a strict vendor management strategy:

  1. Rigorous Risk Assessments: Pinpoint exactly which third parties plug into your enterprise environment, evaluate their baseline security measures (like mandatory Multi-Factor Authentication), and determine your organization’s risk tolerance.
  2. Contractual Accountability: Ensure service level agreements (SLAs) hold third-party vendors legally and financially responsible if their security failures compromise your data.
  3. Independent Compliance Standards: Prioritize partnerships exclusively with vendors that maintain verified cybersecurity certifications—such as SOC 2 Type II or ISO 27001—and require them to share recent penetration testing results.

2. The Explosive Rise of “Shadow AI” Data Leaks

The corporate rush to adopt artificial intelligence has drastically outpaced corporate security guardrails, leading to a massive spike in sensitive data leaks through generative AI programs.

The DBIR found that while 15% of employees regularly use generative AI on corporate devices, the vast majority are bypassing company defenses to do so:

  • 72% accessed these AI platforms using personal, non-corporate email accounts.
  • 17% used corporate emails but entirely lacked integrated corporate authentication systems (such as SAML).

This phenomenon is known as Shadow AI (a highly dangerous subcategory of Shadow IT). Because employees use these platforms to summarize long documents, clean up code, or analyze spreadsheets, they are routinely uploading highly confidential, proprietary corporate data into public AI models that are entirely outside the company’s control.

Steps to Mindfully Safeguard Your AI Era:

  • Codify an Explicit AI Policy: Establish clear rules dictating which AI tools are approved, what types of data are strictly barred from being uploaded, and an emergency protocol for reporting accidental data exposure.
  • Increase Network Visibility: You cannot stop what you cannot see. We deploy advanced monitoring tools that give business leaders deep visibility into user activity, allowing IT teams to spot and intercept unauthorized “Shadow AI” usage instantly.
  • Deploy an AI-Enhanced Security Operations Center (SOC): Cybercriminals are already leveraging generative AI to build hyper-personalized phishing campaigns at scale. To fight fire with fire, your defense infrastructure must utilize AI-driven security tools to detect and neutralize anomalies in real time.

3. Targeted Exploitation of Edge Devices and VPNs

When looking at “initial access vectors”—the literal front door hackers use to break into a business—the DBIR noted an alarming shift away from traditional phishing toward the direct exploitation of network vulnerabilities.

A stunning 22% of these structural exploitation breaches targeted edge devices and Virtual Private Networks (VPNs)—a massive spike from a mere 3% the year prior. Both ransomware networks and espionage-motivated hackers are targeting these network access points with immense success.

The silver lining is that businesses are recognizing the threat; the DBIR highlighted that 54% of discovered edge vulnerabilities were aggressively patched and remediated over the past year.

Rethinking Network Security:

This data completely changes how infrastructure must be configured. For example, when setting up an SD-WAN (Software-Defined Wide Area Network) framework, organizations traditionally focus entirely on network optimization and speed. Given the current threat environment, security must take absolute priority. Edge hardware can no longer just route your traffic; it must act as a fortified, heavily patched firewall.
